From Scaring Ghosts to Sharing Sweets: The Ancient Roots of Halloween

Andrena Yacoub – October 30, 2025 – Food Fun Facts

Halloween might be known today for costumes, candy, and spooky decorations, but its story stretches back over 2,000 years to the Celtic festival of Samhain (pronounced sow-in). Long before carved pumpkins and trick-or-treating, Samhain marked the end of the harvest season and the beginning of winter — a time when people believed the line between the living and the dead grew thin. The Celts, who lived in what is now Ireland, the UK, and northern France, celebrated Samhain around November 1. They believed that on the night before, spirits could cross over into the world of the living. To protect themselves, people lit large bonfires, made offerings to their gods, and wore disguises — often animal skins or masks — to trick wandering spirits into leaving them alone. These early costumes were meant not for fun, but for safety and respect toward supernatural forces.

What makes dim sum so special is the way it brings people together. Families gather on weekends, often filling large round tables with friends, grandparents, and children. There is something deeply communal about passing dishes, sharing stories, and choosing your favorites from rolling carts filled with steaming dumplings, buns, and pastries. You might reach for a shrimp dumpling, a bite of siu mai, or a fluffy char siu bao, but the real magic is in the shared experience. It is food designed for connection, the kind that fills both your stomach and your soul.

Food played a big part in the festival too. After the harvest, people gathered to feast and give thanks for the crops that would sustain them through winter. Offerings of food and drink were also left outside homes to please wandering spirits and ancestors. These customs laid the foundation for many Halloween traditions we know today, from dressing up to sharing treats. As Christianity spread through Europe, the church adapted many pagan festivals into its own calendar. Around the 8th century, Pope Gregory III established November 1 as All Saints’ Day, also known as All Hallows’ Day, to honor saints and martyrs. The night before became known as All Hallows’ Eve — which, over time, shortened to “Halloween.” Despite the new religious meaning, many of the old Samhain customs continued, blending ancient folklore with Christian practices.

The tradition of trick-or-treating can also be traced back to medieval times. On All Hallows’ Eve, poor families and children in parts of Britain and Ireland practiced “souling,” where they went door-to-door offering prayers for the dead in exchange for food or coins. Later, in Scotland and Ireland, children took part in “guising,” dressing up in costumes and performing songs or jokes for treats. When Irish and Scottish immigrants brought these customs to North America in the 19th century, they evolved into the modern practice of trick-or-treating. By the 20th century, Halloween in North America had become a fun, community-centered holiday — focused less on spirits and more on celebration. Candy companies saw an opportunity, and by the 1950s, handing out sweets had become the main event. Pumpkins replaced turnips for carving lanterns, inspired by the old Irish legend of “Stingy Jack,” and costume parties became a tradition for both kids and adults. Today, Halloween is a blend of ancient superstition, cultural adaptation, and modern creativity. From spooky ghost stories to endless candy varieties, it connects us to centuries of history while celebrating imagination and community. Whether you’re scaring ghosts, sharing sweets, or carving a pumpkin, you’re keeping the spirit of Samhain alive — in your own deliciously fun way.
